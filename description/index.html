UNIVERSAL FEATURAL ALPHABET

DESCRIPTION

This is an experimental writing system inspired by IPA, Hangeul, and Artifexian's Oa.
It has no real life use, but I think it's pretty easy to learn, and can be applied to practically any language.
It is written left-to-right, top-to-bottom.
It is written as it is pronounced, so a word like "through" will be written with three symbols, not seven.
All sounds are written in rectangular blocks of constant width. All consonants have a horizontal line at the high level; all vowels have a horizontal line at the mid level.

PULMONIC CONSONANTS

The spelling of every consonant is determined by its place of articulation, its manner of articulation and its voiceness.

PLACE OF ARTICULATION

The places of articulation are as follows: bilabial, labiodental, dental, alveolar, retroflex, postalveolar, palatal, velar, uvular, pharyngeal, glottal. Each is shown as a unique stroke under the horizontal line.

The choice of strokes is to visualise the places of articulation. The leftmost vertical stroke represents lips/teeth, the middle one represents the roof of the mouth, and the rightmost one - the back of the mouth and the larynx.
The whole sequence is a mirror copy of itself, with the palatals in the center. Which is kinda awesome.

Labiovelars are shown as two strokes. There is an additional "lateral" place of articulation, this is mainly intended for clicks, but can also be used to avoid the lateral diacritic in languages which don't want to use it. (More on that later.)

VOICENESS

Most languages have voiceless obstruents (i.e. plosives, affricates and fricatives) and voiced sonorants (i.e. approximants, trills, taps and nasals). Thus, in UFA, a horizontal stroke is drawn in the middle of the consonant to make an obstruent voiced, or to make a sonorant voiceless.

ADDITIONAL DETAILS

Geminates are represented with double strokes for articulation. Half-strokes (secondary articulation or aspiration) don't get doubled. For the purpose of simplifying glyphs, horizontal parts of the stroke don't get doubled as well.

If your language distinguishes palatoalveolars from alveolopalatals, you can represent alveolopalatals as palatalised palatoalveolars.

NON-PULMONIC CONSONANTS

Glottalic consonants, aka ejectives and implosives, are represented with a glottal half-stroke. However, the half-stroke is actually in the bottom half of the glyph, which makes it unlike aspiration. Since languages generally don't use voiceless implosives or voiced ejectives, UFA kinda merges the two categories, distinguishing them using just voiceness.

VOWELS

Vowels are characterised by height and backness, with additional characteristics - such as roundness, length, nasality, rhoticity - in some languages.
Every vowel is represented with a dot. Then, depending on the height and backness of the vowel, there might be additional strokes to the left, right, above or below the dot. In order to differenciate 7 vowel heights, some diagonal strokes are also needed. The table below is a good way to visualise the system.
